# Provenance Guard Planning

## Project Overview

Provenance Guard is a Flask backend API that helps creative platforms estimate whether submitted text was likely written by a human or generated by AI.

Rather than making a binary decision, the system combines multiple detection signals into a confidence score and presents the result using a plain-language transparency label. Every submission is logged in a structured audit log, and creators can appeal decisions they believe are incorrect.

The goal is not perfect AI detection—which is an unsolved problem—but rather to build an honest, transparent system that communicates uncertainty and provides an appeals process.

### Architecture Narrative

When a creator submits text, the API evaluates it using two independent detection signals. The first is an LLM-based judgment using Groq, while the second uses simple stylometric heuristics computed directly in Python.

The scores from both signals are combined into a single confidence score. That confidence score determines both the attribution result and the transparency label shown to users. Every decision is written to the audit log.

If a creator disagrees with the result, they can submit an appeal. The appeal updates the content status to **under_review**, records the creator's reasoning, and is added to the audit log.

# Detection Signals

## Signal 1 — Groq LLM

### What it measures

This signal uses the Groq-hosted Llama model to estimate whether a piece of writing appears more likely to be AI-generated or human-written.

The model considers:

- writing style
- coherence
- tone
- repetition
- overall linguistic patterns

### Output

A score between **0.0 and 1.0**

| Score | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| 0.0 | strongly human |
| 0.5 | uncertain |
| 1.0 | strongly AI |

### Why this signal was chosen

Large language models are capable of evaluating subtle stylistic characteristics that are difficult to express as simple rules.

### What it misses

The LLM may incorrectly classify:

- polished human writing
- professional business writing
- carefully edited AI writing

because these styles often overlap.

---

# Signal 2 — Stylometric Heuristics

The second signal measures objective statistical properties of the text rather than meaning. It computes three lightweight metrics in pure Python, maps each to a 0–1 "AI-likeness" sub-score, and combines them with fixed weights.

| Metric | What it measures | Direction | Weight |
|--------|------------------|-----------|-------:|
| Sentence-length variation (coefficient of variation) | Burstiness — how much sentence length varies | Low variation → AI-like | 0.55 |
| Type-token ratio (windowed to first 100 words) | Vocabulary diversity | Low diversity → AI-like | 0.20 |
| Structural punctuation density (`, ; : — ( ) "` per word) | Use of formal/subordinate punctuation | High density → AI-like | 0.25 |

Sentence-length variation carries the most weight because it is the strongest and most length-robust discriminator; the type-token ratio is windowed to the first 100 words to reduce length sensitivity and is weighted least because it is unreliable on short texts.

### Output

A score between **0.0 and 1.0**

Higher scores indicate writing that appears more AI-like.

Lower scores indicate writing that appears more human-like.

### Why this signal was chosen

This signal is completely independent of the LLM.

Instead of judging meaning, it measures measurable structural properties of the writing.

Using two independent signals makes the final decision more robust than relying on only one.

### What it misses

Stylometric heuristics can incorrectly score:

- academic writing
- legal writing
- technical writing

as AI-generated because those writing styles often have consistent sentence structure and vocabulary.

Likewise, short text may not contain enough information for reliable statistical analysis.

---

# Confidence Scoring and Uncertainty Representation

The final confidence score combines both detection signals using a weighted average.

Formula:

```python
combined_score = (
    0.65 * llm_score +
    0.35 * heuristic_score
)
```

The LLM receives more weight because it captures semantic and stylistic information that simple heuristics cannot.

The heuristic score still contributes because it provides an independent structural signal.

---

## Confidence Thresholds

| Combined Score | Attribution |
|---------------:|-------------|
| 0.70–1.00 | Likely AI |
| 0.31–0.69 | Uncertain |
| 0.00–0.30 | Likely Human |

The uncertain range intentionally covers a wide middle region to reduce false positives against human writers.

Rather than forcing every submission into either “AI-generated” or “human-written,” the system intentionally reserves a broad uncertainty range. This reduces false positives, especially for polished human writing and lightly edited AI content where neither signal is conclusive.

---

# Transparency Label Design

| Attribution | Label Text |
|-------------|------------|
| Likely AI | "This content appears likely to be AI-generated. We are showing this label to provide additional context. The creator may appeal this decision." |
| Uncertain | "We are not confident enough to determine whether this content was written by a human or generated by AI. No final attribution claim is being made." |
| Likely Human | "This content appears likely to be written by a human. No AI-generation label is being shown." |

The labels intentionally avoid technical terms such as "classifier" or "probability" so that non-technical users can easily understand them.

# Appeals Workflow

Creators may contest any attribution result.

Each appeal includes:

- content_id
- creator_reasoning

When an appeal is submitted:

1. The content status changes to **under_review**
2. The creator's explanation is stored
3. The appeal is written to the audit log
4. The API returns confirmation

The project does **not** automatically reclassify content.

Instead, a human reviewer would later examine:

- original text
- both signal scores
- combined confidence
- transparency label
- creator reasoning

before making a final decision.

---

# Rate Limiting

The `/submit` endpoint will use Flask-Limiter.

Limits:

- 10 submissions per minute
- 100 submissions per day

These limits allow normal platform usage while reducing abuse from automated scripts attempting to flood the system.

# Audit Log

The project will use **JSON Lines (.jsonl)** for structured logging.

Each submission creates one log entry.

Example:

```json
{
  "event_type": "submission",
  "content_id": "...",
  "creator_id": "...",
  "timestamp": "...",
  "llm_score": 0.84,
  "heuristic_score": 0.69,
  "confidence": 0.79,
  "attribution": "likely_ai",
  "status": "classified"
}
```

Each appeal creates a second log entry containing:

- timestamp
- content_id
- creator_reasoning
- updated status


# Anticipated Edge Cases

## Edge Case 1 — Highly Polished Human Writing

A skilled human writer may produce text that is grammatically perfect, highly organized, and stylistically consistent. Both the LLM and stylometric heuristic may incorrectly interpret this writing as AI-generated.

**Handling Strategy**

Rather than confidently labeling the content as AI-generated, the system should assign an **uncertain** result whenever the combined confidence score falls within the middle confidence range.

This reduces false positives against legitimate human authors.

---

## Edge Case 2 — Lightly Edited AI Writing

A user may generate text with AI and then modify portions of it by adding personal stories or changing wording.

The LLM and heuristic may disagree:

- the LLM may still recognize AI patterns
- the heuristic may appear more human-like

**Handling Strategy**

The combined confidence score may fall into the uncertain range, allowing the system to communicate uncertainty honestly instead of forcing a binary decision.

---

## Edge Case 3 — Very Short Text

Short submissions such as:

- poems
- quotes
- captions
- headlines

may not provide enough information for either signal to make a reliable decision.

**Handling Strategy**

The system should avoid making strong attribution claims when evidence is limited. Very short text will often receive an uncertain classification.

---

## Edge Case 4 — Technical or Academic Writing

Academic papers, legal writing, and technical documentation often contain:

- repetitive sentence structure
- consistent grammar
- specialized vocabulary

These characteristics may resemble AI-generated writing.

**Handling Strategy**

The weighted confidence score helps reduce overreliance on stylometric heuristics, and uncertain classifications remain available whenever confidence is insufficient.

# Definition of Success

The project will be considered successful if it satisfies the following goals:

- The `/submit` endpoint accepts text and returns structured JSON.
- Two independent detection signals contribute to every classification.
- Confidence scores vary meaningfully across different writing samples.
- All three transparency labels can be produced.
- Appeals update the content status to **under_review**.
- Every submission and appeal is written to a structured audit log.
- Rate limiting prevents excessive submissions.
- The API remains simple, understandable, and easy to extend.

# Summary

Provenance Guard is designed to provide transparent AI attribution rather than perfect detection.

The system intentionally combines two independent signals, communicates uncertainty honestly, provides plain-language transparency labels, records every decision in a structured audit log, and allows creators to appeal classifications they believe are incorrect.

The overall design prioritizes explainability, accountability, and simplicity while satisfying all required Project 4 features.
